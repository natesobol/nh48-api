What is the NH48?

The New Hampshire 48 refers to mountains that meet the AMC Four-Thousand-Footer Club standard of at least 4,000 feet above sea level and 200 feet of topographic prominence. NH48.info is not affiliated with the AMC; this overview summarizes information gathered from public references and hiker contributions.

The idea dates to 1931 when Dartmouth librarian Nathaniel L. Goodrich outlined an early challenge. Refined USGS surveys and AMC guidelines eventually set the list at 48 peaks—adding Bondcliff and Galehead while excluding shoulder summits like Mt. Guyot and Mt. Clay. Readers should confirm criteria and routes with the Appalachian Mountain Club before planning trips.

Peak Difficulty & Strategy

Difficulty is subjective and conditions change quickly. The notes below reflect commonly shared hiker experiences, not official designations. Plan carefully and consult AMC trail guides and forecasts before committing to any route.

Intro / Friendly (≈10 peaks)

Shorter mileage, moderate elevation gain, and generally forgiving terrain.

  • Mt. Tecumseh, Mt. Waumbek, Mt. Hale
  • Mt. Jackson, Mt. Pierce, Mt. Osceola (Tripoli approach)

Moderate Day Hikes (≈15 peaks)

Longer mileage or steeper grades with occasional rough footing.

  • Mt. Garfield, North & South Kinsman
  • Cannon Mountain, North Twin, Cabot

Challenging (≈15 peaks)

Long days, sustained climbs, and rugged sections that test endurance.

  • Owl’s Head, Isolation, Mt. Carrigain
  • Whiteface & Passaconaway, Wildcat Range

Severe / Alpine (≈8 peaks)

Above-treeline exposure, high winds, and the most rugged terrain.

  • Washington, Adams, Jefferson, Madison
  • Franconia Ridge (Lafayette + Lincoln), Bondcliff

Beginner-Friendly choices

  • Mt. Tecumseh — many hikers find this short, direct trail to be the gentlest ascent on the list.
  • Mt. Waumbek — often chosen for its steady grade, sheltered forest, and forgiving footing.
  • Mt. Pierce — provides Crawford Path views with less exposure than some Presidential neighbors.

Advanced & committing

  • The Bonds (Bond, West Bond, Bondcliff) — often tackled as a 19–23 mile day or an overnight traverse.
  • Owl’s Head — known for a long approach with a loose slide and muddy valley miles.
  • The Wildcats — feature relentless grades that many describe as a backcountry stair climber.

Time & Effort Estimates

Total Hiking Time

  • Conservative pace: ~200–250 hours of hiking time.
  • Strong/efficient hikers: ~150 hours on trail.
  • Actual elapsed time will be higher with breaks, overnights, and logistics.

Typical Completion Timeline

  • Fast finish: 1–2 years (or a single intense season).
  • Weekend warrior: 2–4 years for most steady hikers.
  • Casual pace: 4–6+ years with weather and schedules.

“Smart Groupings” – Day-Trip Strategy

The NH48 doesn’t need 48 separate outings. With smart planning, many peaks are commonly paired or traversed in a single day.

Trip Count Targets

  • Minimum logical trips: ~12–15 day hikes for ultra-efficient linkups.
  • Typical total trips: ~20–30 hikes for most people.
  • Average outing: ~8–12 miles, ~3,000–4,000 ft gain.

Classic Combos

  • Pairs: Pierce + Eisenhower, Liberty + Flume, North + South Kinsman.
  • Triples: Tom + Field + Willey, Bondcliff + Bond + West Bond.
  • Traverses: Franconia Ridge Loop, Carter-Moriah Range, Presidential segments.

Easiest-to-Hardest Progression

A progression-based approach helps hikers build skills and confidence. Treat this as guidance, not a rule.

Easiest 10

Start with shorter mileage and forgiving trails.

Tecumseh, Waumbek, Hale, Jackson, Pierce, Osceola and other low-commitment peaks.

Middle 20

Longer hikes and moderate combos build endurance.

Garfield, Kinsmans, Hancocks, Cannon, Wildcats and similar mid-range efforts.

Hardest 10

Save the most rugged and exposed summits for last.

Washington, Adams, Jefferson, Madison, Bonds, Isolation, Owl’s Head.

Risk & Preparedness Stats

Exposure & Weather

  • ~15 peaks include above-treeline alpine exposure.
  • Presidential summits are famous for rapid weather swings and extreme wind.

Remote, No-Bailout Days

  • Owl’s Head and The Bonds often exceed 18–20 miles round-trip.
  • Isolation is a long out-and-back with limited exit options.

Water Planning

  • Several routes have no reliable water beyond trailheads.
  • Plan to carry 2–3 liters on exposed or remote trips.

Completion Strategy Highlights

There is no single “right” way to finish the NH48. Pick the approach that matches your goals and schedule.

Fast-Track Plan

~12 high-efficiency hikes combining multiple peaks per outing.

Expect big traverses like Presidential Range days and a Pemi-style loop.

Weekend Warrior

~20–30 trips, averaging 2 peaks per hike.

Finishes in roughly 2–4 years with steady weekend hikes.

Scenic & Slow

~30–40 hikes with flexible pacing and weather chasing.

Prioritize prime conditions and classic loops over efficiency.

History & Rules Recap

The AMC Four-Thousand-Footer Club launched officially in 1957 to encourage hikers into the deeper corners of the White Mountain National Forest. According to AMC guidelines, a peak must have 200 feet of prominence from any connecting col. Human-powered ascents are expected—using the Cog Railway or Cannon’s tramway does not meet the guidelines.

Thousands of finishers log their climbs by mailing the AMC application or joining online trackers. Please read the Appalachian Mountain Club’s introductory letter for the most accurate and current requirements, and verify any summary here with the official resource.

Frequently Asked Questions

These responses summarize common hiker opinions and may not reflect official AMC guidance. For definitive answers, visit the official AMC FAQ.

What is the easiest 4,000 footer in New Hampshire?

Mt. Tecumseh and Mt. Waumbek are widely considered the most approachable thanks to short mileage, steady grades, and sheltered routes; however, experiences vary.

Do winter ascents count toward the regular NH48 list?

Many hikers count winter ascents toward the primary list and separately for the Winter NH48 award. December 21–March 21 is the commonly accepted window; review AMC criteria to be sure.

Which range has the most challenging weather?

The Presidential Range is often cited for challenging weather. Above-treeline exposure, fast-moving storms, and wind chills make even summer days serious—plan conservatively.

Use the /peak/slug/ format shown in the Current NH 4,000-Footer List. Each peak page includes a “Back to the NH48 Info” link for reciprocal navigation.
